# Indian Stock Red Flags — Governance & Quality Issues

Indian markets have unique risks that don't always show up in P&L statements. This guide covers what to watch for.

## 🚨 High-severity red flags (avoid the stock)

### Promoter pledging > 30-40%
When promoters pledge shares to take personal loans, they're essentially betting the company. If the stock price falls, lenders can sell the pledged shares, causing a downward spiral. This has destroyed many stocks (DHFL, Subhash Projects, etc.).
- **How to check**: Screener.in shareholding section, or NSE/BSE shareholding pattern filings
- **Threshold**: Any pledging needs an explanation; > 30% is alarming; > 50% is almost always avoid

### Auditor red flags
- **Frequent auditor changes**: If the company switches auditors every 2-3 years, management may be shopping for auditors who'll sign off on aggressive accounting
- **Resignation letters with reasons**: When auditors resign and cite specific concerns in their letter, take it very seriously
- **Unknown/small audit firm for a large company**: A ₹5,000 crore company using a 3-person audit firm is suspicious
- **Qualified audit opinion**: Any qualifications in the auditor's report are a serious red flag

### Related Party Transactions (RPTs)
High RPTs between the listed company and promoter-owned private companies are often used to siphon funds.
- **How to spot**: Annual report's "Related Party Disclosures" section
- **Red: Loans given to promoter group companies; Sales to promoter companies at below-market rates; Rent paid to promoter property companies at above-market rates

### Cash flow doesn't match profits
The oldest accounting trick is booking revenue without actually collecting cash.
- Net Profit is ₹500 crore but Operating Cash Flow is only ₹200 crore → where's the cash?
- Receivables growing much faster than revenue → they're booking sales that haven't been paid for
- Classic fraud signal in Indian markets (Satyam, Manpasand Beverages, NDTV)

### Promoter holding consistently falling (without explanation)
- Slow but steady promoter selling = promoters don't believe in their own company's future
- Acceptable reason: Regulatory dilution requirement (banks, etc.)
- Not acceptable reason: No communication at all

## ⚠️ Medium-severity yellow flags (investigate further)

### High debt in cyclical businesses
- Infrastructure, real estate, metals, textiles can have high debt
- Debt is dangerous in cyclical downturns (IL&FS, JSPL in 2015)
- For cyclicals: look at Net Debt / EBITDA. If > 3x, it's risky.

### Aggressive revenue recognition
- Long-term contract companies (EPC, construction) can front-load revenue
- Check if revenue growth and cash flow growth match over 5+ years

### Promoter group buying via creeping acquisition
- Promoters buying shares from the open market isn't always bad, but if done with borrowed funds...
- Check if promoter buying coincides with pledging going up

### Very high accounts receivable
- Debtor days > 90 in a business that doesn't warrant it = collection issues
- Especially dangerous in B2B businesses (Manpasand Beverages had this before it collapsed)

### Subsidiary complexity
- Too many subsidiaries (50+ for a mid-size company) can hide losses or enable tunneling
- Offshore subsidiaries in tax havens with no clear business purpose

### Frequent QIPS / Equity dilution
- Companies that raise equity every 1-2 years are burning cash
- Dilutive for existing shareholders
- Question: If the business is profitable, why do they keep needing fresh equity?

### Management compensation way above industry norms
- If the promoter-MD takes a salary of ₹50 crore/year from a company earning ₹200 crore, that's extracting value
- Check the "Related Party Disclosures" in annual reports for total management remuneration

## ✅ Positive governance signals

These indicate a well-run, trustworthy management:

- **Zero promoter pledging for years** — consistently clean
- **Buybacks at attractive valuations** — management thinks stock is undervalued, returns cash to shareholders
- **Dividends even in bad years** — company has genuine cash flows, not just accounting profits
- **Promoter increasing stake from open market** — personal conviction
- **Consistent, predictable results** — no wild surprises, honest guidance
- **Founder-led businesses with long track record** — skin in the game, reputation at stake
- **Long-tenured auditor (Big 4 or reputable mid-size firm)** — Deloitte, EY, KPMG, PWC, BSR & Co., S.R. Batliboi, Walker Chandiok
- **ESOP grants to employees (not just promoters)** — aligns employee incentives
- **Annual report that clearly discusses risks and failures** — honest management talks about what went wrong, not just what went right

## High-profile Indian accounting frauds to learn from

1. **Satyam Computers (2009)**: Fictitious cash balances of ₹5,000 crore. Auditor (PwC India) missed it or ignored it.
2. **DHFL (2019)**: Diversion of thousands of crores to promoter shell companies via fake loans.
3. **Manpasand Beverages (2019)**: Revenue fabrication; auditor resigned citing concerns.
4. **Amtek Auto / JSPL (2015-16)**: Excessive debt in cyclical businesses; governance failures.
5. **PC Jeweller (2017-19)**: Promoter pledging + questionable accounting.
6. **Reliance Capital / Reliance Home Finance (2019-22)**: Promoter siphoning, auditor concerns, IL&FS contagion.

**Pattern**: In almost every case, some combination of high promoter pledging, frequent auditor changes, cash-profit mismatch, and very high related party transactions were present years before the collapse. Screener.in's 10-year data would have shown the warning signs.

## Quick screening checklist before investing

Before buying any Indian stock, run through this:

- [ ] Promoter pledging = 0% or clearly explained
- [ ] CFO / Net Profit > 70% over 5 years (check Screener cash flow)
- [ ] ROCE > 15% consistently over 5-10 years
- [ ] Debt/Equity < 0.7 (unless banking/NBFC — different metric applies)
- [ ] No auditor changes in last 5 years
- [ ] Promoter holding stable or increasing
- [ ] No obvious RPT red flags in annual report
- [ ] Revenue and cash flow growth roughly in sync
